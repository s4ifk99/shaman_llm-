#  How to start a small claim or fast track claim without a solicitor

Don't be put off by the court process. Our expert guide empowers you to start your small claim (up to £10k) or fast track case (£10k–£25k) correctly using either Money Claims (OCMC), or the N1 form. We explain what to put in each box and what to consider for different types of cases.

The information in this guide applies to England and Wales.

This guide explains how to start your claim if you have decided to take someone to the small claims court, or use the fast-track process in a county court, without the help of a solicitor. It is part of a [series of guides](/get-help/going-court/small-claims-and-civil-court "Small claims and the civil court") that explain everything you need to know and understand if you are taking someone to the civil court to sort out a dispute worth £25,000 or less.

##  Is this guide for you?

Open

Close

This guide is for you if:

  * you have decided to start a court claim against an individual or an organisation in either England or Wales, and
  * your case involves a claim for £25,000 or less, and
  * you are representing yourself (you are a litigant in person), and
  * you are not eligible to have your case paid for by legal aid, a trade union, or insurance.

This guide is also for people supporting litigants in person, for example Support Through Court volunteers, Citizens’ Advice volunteers, housing support workers, advice workers and court staff, as well as relatives and friends.

It will not help you if your case is:

  * a criminal case,
  * a family case (such as an application for a domestic violence injunction or a divorce – see [Going to the family court](/get-help/family-and-children/going-family-court "Going to the family court") instead),
  * a housing disrepair or housing possession case including mortgage possession (go to [help with housing problems](/node/9259) instead),
  * an injunction (including court claims about anti-social behaviour)
  * a medical accident case,
  * a case involving defamation (libel or slander) or
  * a tribunal case (such as a discrimination or employment case –  see [Going to a tribunal](/get-help/going-court "Going to court") instead).

If you are considering making a claim about injuries caused by a car accident claim that happened on or after 31st May 2021 please ensure you read [Make a small claim about injuries caused by a car accident](/get-help/going-court/small-claims-and-civil-court/make-small-claim-about-injuries-caused-car "Make a small claim about injuries caused by a car accident") first. That takes you through all the steps you must take before you start legal proceedings (what lawyers call the pre-action protocol).

There are special rules dealing with situations where you don’t or can’t follow standard practice for whatever reason. For example, you think you’re ready to start a civil claim but you haven’t got your defendant’s address, or your defendant lives abroad. We cover some of these briefly but you will probably need specialist advice to help you work out what to do in your particular situation.

##  Starting your claim

Open

Close

There are different ways to start your claim. You can now start lots of claims online.

For many people the new online process is easier, as it was designed to be used by people who do not have a solicitor. Cases using the online system are also frequently resolved faster and sometimes the court fees are slightly lower.

You do not need to use the online system if you don’t want to. If you don’t feel confident or comfortable dealing with things online, it might be better for you to use the paper system. You don’t want extra things to worry about.



If you are considering making a claim about injuries caused by a car accident that happened on or after 31st May 2021 please ensure you read [How to make a small claim about injuries caused by a car accident](/get-help/going-court/small-claims-and-civil-court/make-small-claim-about-injuries-caused-car "Make a small claim about injuries caused by a car accident") first.



In order to know which service to use you need to know the rough value of your claim. There is information about valuing your claim in another guide in this series. Take a look at [Should I sue someone?](/get-help/going-court/small-claims-and-civil-court/should-i-sue-someone "Should I sue someone? Help to decide whether to take legal action")

### 1) Money Claims

This is a new digital service designed to be used by people who do not have a solicitor to help them (often called Litigants in Person). It is also called Online Civil Money Claims or OCMC. You can now use it to make a claim of up to £25,000 (including interest), if your claim started from 8 October 2024 (for cases issued before 8 October 2024, the limit is £10,000 or less), or respond to a claim started by someone else. These are called ‘small claims’. You cannot use it if your claim is about anything other than money.

You cannot use this service:

  * if you are under 18, or don’t have an address in the UK
  * if you are eligible for legal aid,
  * if you don’t know exactly how much to claim for,
  * to make a claim against more than one person or organisation
  * to make a personal injury claim
  * to bring a claim against a government department/agency,
  * to get your tenancy deposit back from your landlord,
  * to make a claim against a person or organisation based outside England and Wales, or
  * to bring a claim against someone without mental capacity or under 18.

You will need:

  * a debit or credit card to pay the court fees (unless you are entitled to [help with your court fee](/get-help/going-court/small-claims-and-civil-court/how-apply-help-civil-court-fees "How to apply for help with civil court fees") that pays your entire fee),
  * an address in the UK, and
  * an email address and regular access to a computer and the internet.

You can use this system if you are entitled to help with paying your court fees but you must apply for that help online ([Make a claim](/node/12423 "Make a court claim for money") shows you how to do this). Make sure you keep your reference number safe for the [Help with fees](/node/12673 "Apply for help with fees") application safe as you will need it before your claim can be officially started.

As part of the Money Claim service, if the case is valued at under £10,000, free telephone mediation to help you come to an agreed solution is now mandatory (you have to do it). If it is over £10,000 you can choose whether or not to try it. In this kind of mediation, a trained mediator speaks to both sides separately over the phone and helps them to come to a legally binding agreement (which often includes a payment plan).

If you are able to come to an agreement using mediation, the case will be over (and you might receive your money) much more quickly, and neither side will have to pay a hearing fee. You will usually speak to a mediator within 2-3 weeks, and are likely to have to wait 30 weeks for a court date.

You can find out more about it and start your claim at [Make a court claim for money](/node/12423 "Make a court claim for money").

### 2) Money Claim Online (MCOL)

This is another online service run by HM Courts & Tribunals service. It was not originally designed to be used by people who don’t have a solicitor to help them (although you can) and as a result is less user friendly.

You can use [Money Claim Online](/node/9959 "Money claim online") to start your claim:

  * if you know the exact amount you are claiming (which excludes all personal injury cases) and that amount is under £100,000
  * if there are two defendants or less, and
  * if your claim is against a person or organisation with an address in England or Wales.

The same exclusions apply as for the Money Claim described above. [Money Claims Online](/node/9959 "Money claim online") is therefore most useful if you are claiming an amount that is over the £10,000 upper limit you can use Money Claims for.

You will need:

  * a debit or credit card to pay the court fees,
  * an address in the UK, and
  * an email address and regular access to a computer and the internet.

You can find out more about this service at Money Claim Online.

The Money Claims Online process is like the N1 paper form. Follow the guidance in Completing the N1 paper claim form below.

One of the advantages of the Money Claims Online process over the paper form is that it automatically includes a claim for the court fee you will have to pay to start the case, and asks if you want to claim interest on the amount - although you have to calculate the amount of interest yourself.



### Forms and rules

You can find guidance about Money Claim Online (MCOL) at [Practice direction 7C - Money Claim Online](/node/15777 "Practice direction 7C - Money Claim Online")

### 3) Claim by post using an N1 paper form

Lots of people still use the paper form to start their claim. Currently, it is the only way you can make a claim for personal injury.  You also need to use the paper form if you don’t exactly how much to claim for.

To start your claim you will need to complete an N1 claim form, attach the necessary documents and the court fee (or an application for [help with fees](/node/12673 "Apply for help with fees")) and send it to the right court. You can find English, Welsh and large print versions of form N1 together with notes (N1A) explaining how to complete this form at [GOV.UK](/node/14462 "Form N1: Make a claim against a person or organisation \(Claim form CPR Part 7\)").

##  Completing the N1 paper claim form

Open

Close

You might want to claim using the paper form because you are not comfortable with computers, don’t have an email address, or are making a type of claim not yet possible to do using the online service. You may also need to use the paper form if you don’t currently know the exact amount you are claiming for.

The claim form is a very important document. If you make a mistake on the form, you claim may fail, or you may have to pay an additional fee to correct the mistake.

If you are claiming for personal injury and relying on the evidence of a medical practitioner, you will need to attach a medical report about your injuries to your claim form. You must also attach a schedule of loss, listing your losses and any future expenses you are claiming (explained below).

We explain what to include in key sections of form N1 below.



### Court rules

The relevant rules about what to include in or with your N1 claim form are

[Part 16 and Practice Direction 16 - Statements of case](/node/14465 "Part 16 - Statements of case")

### The defendant

It is important to get things like the details of who you are claiming against correct. Please read [Who to sue?](/get-help/going-court/small-claims-and-civil-court/who-sue "Who to sue in a civil court?") for advice about identifying the right defendant to take your claim against and how to find the correct address for them. Make sure you have their name exactly right. This can be particularly tricky if it is an organisation you are taking a claim against.

### Valuing an injury

To value an injury, you will need to get a medical report which sets out the details of the injury (or injuries) and the prognosis (when and to what extent you will make a recovery).

The notes for guidance (N1A) include some help on how to word what you might want to say about the value of your claim.

### Where to send your claim

If you want your claim dealt with by the county court and your claim is just for money (this includes claims for compensation for personal injury) then you must send it to the:

Civil National Business Centre
St Katharine’s House
21-27 St Katharine’s Street
Northampton
NN1 2LH

In these cases, you enter ‘In the Civil National Business Centre’ in the heading on the form (or you can abbreviate to CNBC).If your claim is for anything other than money, then you should send it to your local court office. See [Things you need to know about court procedure before you sue](/get-help/going-court/small-claims-and-civil-court/things-you-need-know-about-court-procedure-you "Pre-action protocols - What you need to know before starting a civil claim") for more information about how to find your local court.

### Checklist – what you need to put together to post to the court

  * Form N1,
  * Particulars of claim (if separate),
  * Medical report (if claiming for personal injury),
  * Schedule of loss (if claiming for losses and expenses),
  * Cheque for the court fee, or application for Help with Fees if eligible, or both, and
  * A complete set of copies for each defendant.

When you post the documents to the court, it is worth getting a certificate of posting from the post office. Then, if necessary, you can prove when you posted them.

You will know whether you have started your claim successfully when the court sends you a Notice of Issue.

##  Informing the defendant

Open

Close

Next, you need to give the defendant all the details about the court proceedings in a particular way. The law calls this ‘serving the claim on the defendant’. There are lots of special rules about how and when it must be done, and it is easy to make a mistake.

If you include the particulars of claim in the relevant box on your N1 claim form, or attach a separate particulars of claim document to the claim form, the court will ‘serve’ your claim on the defendant for you. This is the best and least worrying way of managing your case and will save you a lot of bother. It is what happens in most cases.

If you don’t include the particulars of claim in the N1 claim form, you have 14 days after the court has sent the defendant the claim form to deliver the particulars of claim to the Defendant, together with the correct response pack. The court won’t do this for you - you have to do it yourself.

If you are serving all of the claim documents yourself, you have up to 4 calendar months from the date the case officially started (‘issued’) to formally deliver the N1 Claim Form and the Particulars of Claim to the defendant. (You have more time to deliver the Particulars of Claim if the Defendant is outside England and Wales. You can find the relevant court rules about this at Practice direction 6B – Service out of the jurisdiction)



### Warning!

The formal delivery (‘service’) of court documents is a tricky area of law. There are lots of special rules about how and when it must be done. It is easy to make a mistake. If you don’t do it in the correct way and at the right time, you have a big problem. A lot can go wrong at this early stage if you are running out of time, and you may then need some legal advice.

The time limit for this formal delivery of your claim is very strict, and if you miss it there is practically no chance of retrieving the situation.

By far the better option is to include all the details on your N1 form, so the court can “serve” the documents for you.



Rules
Relevant rule:  [Part 6 - Serving of documents](/node/14477 "Part 6 - Serving of documents")

### Stopping a claim

If you regret starting the claim, or the problem is resolved another way, you can stop the claim at any time. You do this either through Money Claims or by sending a completed ‘notice of discontinuance’ (Form N279) to the court and formally delivering a copy every side in the case.

But you will be responsible for everyone’s legal costs up until the date you give them this notice, unless you can convince the court that you don’t have to pay. This is not easy to do. Try contacting your opponent before you complete the ‘notice of discontinuance’ and ask them to agree that they will not claim for their costs if you stop your claim. You never know, you might get lucky.

If your case has been allocated to the small claims track before you stop the claim, then usually you will only have to pay your own legal costs and not everyone else’s as well.

##  After you have successfully issued your claim

Open

Close

### The paper service

The court sends a copy of your Claim Form (and the Particulars of Claim if they are in a separate document and any other documents) to the defendant as well as some notes to help the defendant decide what to do about it. [Notes for defendant on replying to the claim form (N1C)](/node/12418 "Notes for defendant on replying to the claim form \(N1C\)") are helpful. Welsh and large print version are also available.

The defendant also gets sent a response pack ([Form N9](/node/15785 "Form N9: Ask for more time to file a defence \('acknowledgment of service'\)")). The response pack explains what to do next and includes the forms the defendant needs. If your Claim Form includes the Particulars of Claim then this pack is sent at the same time as your Claim Form. If you deliver your Particulars of Claim separately, you must send the response pack to the defendant at the same time.

### Money Claim service

If you have supplied an email address for the defendant, they will be told that a legal claim has been started against them and will receive a copy of the claim form straight away. A copy is also sent to them in the post.

The defendant then has the choice of whether to use the online service, [respond to court claim for money](/node/15268 "Respond to a court claim for money"), or on paper as above. They have 28 days to respond.

If the defendant needs a bit more time to respond, they can ask the claimant to agree to for an additional 28 days. They do not need to have a reason.  If an agreement is made the defendant can update the online service and the new agreed date will be registered on the system.  This must be done within the first 28 days. If it is not, the defendant will need to make an application to the court for more time to respond.

##  If the defendant does not respond

Open

Close

If the defendant has not responded to your claim within the time limit set by the court you can ask the court to decide the case anyway. The law calls this applying for ‘judgment in default’.

They are likely to receive a County Court Judgement (CCJ) against them which will harm their credit rating for a long time.

If they agree that they owe the money they should still respond to your claim. It is still possible to settle the claim out of court. See[ How to settle a civil claim](/get-help/going-court/small-claims-and-civil-court/how-settle-civil-claim "How to settle a civil claim") for more help.

##  If the defendant does respond

Open

Close

In responding to your claim, the defendant will:

  * admit (accept) they owe you the amount of money claimed, or
  * admit part of the claim (which means agree that they owe you some of the money claimed, but disagree with the rest), or
  * reject all of your claim (which means they disagree that they owe you any more money).

If they accept that they owe you money (either all of the amount claimed, or part of it), they are asked to pay the full amount within 5 days.If they do not accept, you can ask for a County Court Judgement (CCJ) to be made against them. Having a CCJ will hurt their credit rating and will make it harder to get loans, a mobile phone contract, or a mortgage.

They can, however, ask if you will accept payment by a set date or allow them to pay in instalments.

If they don’t accept that they owe all or some of the money you claimed, you should read their defence carefully, as you may need to reassess the strength and value of your claim in light of what they say.

If they believe that they also lost out, and that you therefore owe them money, they may also begin a counter-claim (a claim against you).

##  FAQs about starting a small claim or fast track case

Open

Close

### What is the difference between a small claim and a fast track claim?

The Small Claims Track is for claims valued up to £10,000, with very limited recoverable costs. The Fast Track is for claims valued between £10,000 and £25,000, and uses the Fixed Recoverable Costs (FRC) regime. See[ Legal costs and who pays them](/get-help/going-court/small-claims-and-civil-court/legal-costs-and-who-pays-them "Legal costs and who pays them") for more details.

### How much does it cost to start a civil claim in England and Wales?

The initial court fee is variable, calculated as a percentage of your claim's value, or a fixed fee for claims under £300. This is one reason it’s important to work out how much your claim is worth correctly. See [Should i sue someone?](/get-help/going-court/small-claims-and-civil-court/should-i-sue-someone "Should I sue someone? Help to decide whether to take legal action") to understand how to value your case.  You may be able to get help with fees through Help with fees. See [Help with court fees in a civil or family case](/get-help/family-and-children/family-court/help-court-fees-civil-or-family-case "How to apply for help with family court fees") for more information.

### How long does a small claim or fast track case take?

Court backlogs mean cases are taking longer. At the moment a Small Claim takes approximately 40-50 weeks to reach trial, if it goes that far. A Fast Track case is longer, taking approximately 60-80 weeks from the time you start the case to trial, if it is not settled before then.

### Do I need a solicitor to represent me in a small claim case?

No. The small claims track is designed to be accessible for people a solicitor. But getting expert information, like this Advicenow guide, is strongly advised.

### What are the key steps to start a court case yourself?

  1. Be sure you have a good case, have all the evidence you need, and that it will be worth it. Read [Should I sue someone?](/get-help/going-court/small-claims-and-civil-court/should-i-sue-someone "Should I sue someone? Help to decide whether to take legal action") for more details.
  2. Try to sort out the dispute another way first.
  3. Check you are within the time limit. See [How long do you have to sue someone? Time limits explained](/get-help/going-court/small-claims-and-civil-court/time-limits-suing-someone "How long do you have to sue someone? Time limits explained") for more details.
  4. Check that your defendant will be able to pay you if you win. See [Find out if the defendant is worth suing](/get-help/going-court/small-claims-and-civil-court/find-out-if-defendant-worth-suing "Find out if the defendant is worth suing") for more help.
  5. Work out who to name as the defendant. This is sometimes tricker than it sounds and a mistake can be costly. See[ Who to sue in a civil court](/get-help/going-court/small-claims-and-civil-court/who-sue "Who to sue in a civil court?") for more details.
  6. Be sure you understand the costs and if you might have to pay some of the other side's costs if you lose. See [Legal costs and who has to pay them](https://Legal costs and who has to pay them).
  7. Make sure you follow the correct procedure before you start the court case, including sending a letter before action. See [Things you need to know about court procedure before you sue someone](/get-help/going-court/small-claims-and-civil-court/things-you-need-know-about-court-procedure-you "Pre-action protocols - What you need to know before starting a civil claim").
  8. Use Money claims (the new online service) or complete the N1 form and pay the court fee to start the case. This guide explains what you have to do,  step-by-step.

##  What does it mean?

Open

Close

Brief details of claim \- a concise statement of the nature of your claim and the remedy you want, for example, a payment of money.

Claim form – the form you use to start your claim.

Claim Notification Form – the form you use to notify the other side of your claim.

Discontinuance – ending the case before the trial.

Expert evidence \- this is evidence of an expert’s opinion, of what they think or believe about something.

General damages – the amount awarded for the injury itself.

Issue proceedings – the official start of the court case

Liability \- proving that the problem is legally the defendant’s fault (they breached your legal rights or breached a contract with you).

Litigant in person \- a person bringing or defending a claim without a solicitor or barrister.

Loss of amenity – means not being able to do the things, including leisure activities, which you would normally do. The award covers physical and mental injury.

Low value personal injury claims – these are claims where the personal injury element of the claim is worth over £1,000 and the overall value of the claim is worth under £25,000.

Money claim – the online HMCTS service that you can use to issue claims for a fixed amount of money (currently only for claims under £10,000 unless both sides are represented). Its is also called Online Civil Money Claims (OCMC).

Money claim online (MCOL) – the online service that can be used to start a claim for a specific amount of money up to £10,000.

Medical report – a report from a recognised medical practitioner prepared in support of your claim for compensation for personal injury.

Notice of discontinuance – the form you use to tell the court that you want to stop (discontinue) your claim.

Notice of Issue – this tells you that your claim has started and the date it began. It also tells you the case number, the date of service, the method of service and the defendant’s deadline for responding.

Pain – how much it hurts.

Particulars of Claim – a concise written statement of the facts and law on which your claim is based and what you want from the defendant.

Remedy –  what the court can order to rectify the problem, usually money compensation.

Respond to a money claim – the online HMCTS service that you can use to respond to a money claim started using Make a money claim.

Response pack (Form N9) – this explains what the defendant has to do after they receive a claim form and includes the forms the defendant may need.

Schedule of loss – a document listing the losses and expenses you’ve had as a result of the defendant’s conduct.

Service/Serving documents – the formal delivery of court documents to others involved in the case so that they can respond. There are lots of special rules about how and when it must be done. If you make a mistake or do not do it within the deadline, it may end your case.

Special damages \- past and future expenses and losses - for example, loss of earnings, travel costs, help with childcare.

Suffering – mental anguish which doesn’t amount to a mental illness.

Quantum – the amount of compensation your claim is worth.

When someone close to you passes away, dealing with their estate can be overwhelming. That's where [probate](https://lawhive.co.uk/knowledge-hub/wills-trust-probate/what-is-probate-and-how-does-it-work/) comes in. Probate is the process of sorting out the deceased person's property, money, and possessions. It's a way of ensuring their wishes are respected, and their affairs are wrapped up.

Handling probate independently can save you money and give you more control over the process. However, it can be complex and time-consuming. This guide will walk you through how to get probate without a solicitor. We'll cover:

  * When you need to apply for probate
  * The probate application process
  * Common challenges in applying for probate independently
  * Benefits and drawbacks of handling probate independently
  * When to consider using a solicitor

### When Do You Need to Apply for Probate?

[Probate is necessary](https://lawhive.co.uk/knowledge-hub/wills-trust-probate/do-i-need-a-solicitor-for-probate/) when the deceased owned property in their sole name, such as a house or flat. It's also required when they have significant financial assets, like savings, investments, or shares. However, probate may not be required if the estate is small (typically under £5,000). In addition, if the deceased owned assets jointly with someone else, such as a spouse or partner, those assets usually pass automatically to the co-owner without probate.

If the deceased left a will, you must apply for a Grant of Probate. This document confirms the executor's authority to manage the estate according to the will. On the other hand, if there's no will, you'll need a Grant of Letters of Administration. This grant allows the administrator to distribute the estate according to the law.

### How to Apply for Probate Without a Solicitor

If you're looking to learn how to apply for probate without a solicitor, we've broken it down below. This is our quick and easy guide of how to do it yourself.

#### 1\. Determine If You Need Probate

The first thing to do is figure out if you need probate. To determine if probate is required, list the deceased's assets and estimate their value. You can get help from HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) or a probate helpline if you're unsure.

#### 2\. Gather Necessary Documents

Now that you know probate is necessary, it's time to gather the documents you'll need for your DIY probate application. To prevent delays, ensure you have all the necessary documents before moving on to the next step. These usually include:

  * The original will (if there is one)
  * The death certificate
  * A completed inheritance tax form (IHT205 or IHT400, depending on the estate's value)
  * Property deeds
  * Share certificates
  * Other relevant documents

#### 3\. Complete the Probate Application Form

Next, fill out the probate application form. There are two types: PA1P (for estates with a will) and PA1A (for estates without a will). Take your time and fill out the form carefully, then double-check for errors and sign it. Pay attention to the following sections:

  * Section 5: Executor's Details (your name, address, and contact info)
  * Section 7: Estate Assets (list all assets, including property, savings, and investments)
  * Section 10: Inheritance Tax (calculate any tax owed)

#### 4\. Pay the Probate Application Fee

The probate fee in the UK currently costs £273 for estates over £5,000 and is free for estates under £5,000. You'll also need to pay £1.50 for each additional copy of the probate document. You can learn more about the financials in our guide to [probate fees paid from the estate](https://lawhive.co.uk/knowledge-hub/wills-trust-probate/can-probate-fees-be-paid-from-the-estate/).

#### 5\. Submit Your Application

This is the last step in the probate application process UK. Finally, submit your application online or by post to the Probate Registry. Online applications are recommended because they are usually processed faster. Keep a copy of your application and ensure you receive receipt confirmation from the Probate Registry.

### Common Challenges in Applying for Probate Independently

Applying for probate on your own can be a daunting task due to the complexities involved. Understanding these challenges and seeking the right resources can help simplify the process and reduce the risk of errors, making the probate application smoother and more efficient.

#### Complex Forms and Legal Jargon

One of the biggest challenges is dealing with complex forms and legal jargon. Probate forms can be confusing, especially if you're unfamiliar with legal terminology. The forms are lengthy and require precise information, which can be overwhelming. To overcome this, start by visiting the UK Government's website, which offers helpful guides and resources on probate. You can also contact the Probate Helpline on 0300 123 1072 for guidance. In addition, consider using free legal advice services, such as Citizens Advice or local law clinics.

#### Delays and Rejections

Another common issue is delays and rejections due to application mistakes. This can significantly extend the time it takes to receive the grant of probate. Double-check your application carefully before submission to minimise errors. Make sure you have accurate asset valuations and beneficiary details and use online resources or seek advice if you're unsure. A simple mistake can cause significant delays, so it's essential to be meticulous.

#### Dealing with Inheritance Tax

Dealing with inheritance tax can also pose a challenge. You'll need to complete the correct inheritance tax form (IHT205 or IHT400) and pay any tax due before submitting your probate application. Inheritance tax must be paid before probate is granted, so it's crucial to get this right. Consider consulting HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) or a tax advisor to ensure accurate tax calculations. Keep records of tax payments and correspondence with HMRC, which will help resolve any issues quickly.

### Benefits and Drawbacks of Handling Probate Independently

Handling probate on your own can save money and give you control over the process. However, it also comes with challenges like dealing with complex forms and the risk of delays or errors. Weighing these pros and cons can help you decide if it's the right approach for you.

#### Top 2 Benefits of Handling Probate Independently

  1. Cost Saving: Handling probate independently saves you money because you avoid solicitors' fees, which typically range from 1-5% of the estate's value. When you take charge, you keep more of the estate's assets for the beneficiaries.
  2. Control Over The Process: You also stay in control of the process and make informed decisions that ensure everything happens according to the deceased's wishes. This control allows you to manage the pace of the process, moving it as quickly or slowly as needed.

#### Main 2 Drawbacks of Handling Probate Independently

  1. Time and Effort: Handling probate independently requires significant time and effort. You must manage complex paperwork, numerous forms, and strict deadlines, which can overwhelm you, especially during an already difficult time.
  2. Risk of Making Mistakes: If you don't carefully complete the application or paperwork, you risk making costly mistakes. These errors may result in delays, increased costs, or even court involvement, causing significant stress and anxiety that can lead to [probate disputes](https://lawhive.co.uk/wills-trust-probate/probate-dispute/) among beneficiaries.

### When to Consider Using a Solicitor

Handling probate independently can save money, but some situations require professional expertise.

#### Complex Estates

If the estate is large or complex, it's wise to seek help from a solicitor. This is especially true if the estate includes foreign assets, as a solicitor will better understand international law. Disputes among beneficiaries or contested will also call for professional assistance. A solicitor will help resolve these issues fairly and efficiently. Similarly, if the deceased had business interests or partnerships, a solicitor's expertise will navigate these complexities.

#### Lack of Time or Expertise

Lack of time or expertise is another reason to consider hiring a solicitor for your [probate application](https://lawhive.co.uk/wills-trust-probate/probate-application/). If you're the executor or administrator, you may not have the time or confidence to handle the process effectively. A solicitor will handle the paperwork, communicate with authorities, and ensure timely completion. If you're unsure about the process or feel overwhelmed, don't hesitate to seek help.

### 3 Best Practices for a Smooth Probate Application

Applying for probate can be complex, but following a few best practices can make it smoother.

#### 1\. Stay Organised

Maintain a checklist of tasks to complete and include deadlines and key milestones to ensure everything stays on track. Keep all documents and correspondence related to the estate in one secure location, such as a file or binder. This includes:

  1. The will and any codicils
  2. Death certificate
  3. Probate application forms
  4. Correspondence with the Probate Registry
  5. Asset valuations and accounts

#### 2\. Seek Guidance When Needed

Don't be afraid to ask for help when you need it. There are many free resources available to guide you through the probate process. Government websites and charitable organisations like Citizens Advice, Lawhive, and local law clinics offer valuable information and support. You can also contact the Probate Helpline for guidance. These resources can help you navigate complex issues and avoid costly mistakes.

#### 3\. Communicate with Beneficiaries

Providing regular updates to beneficiaries is essential to avoid misunderstandings or disputes. Schedule regular meetings or updates to keep them informed about the progress of the probate process. This openness ensures everyone is on the same page and manages expectations. Be transparent about timelines, decisions, and any challenges that arise. Clear communication helps build trust and reduces stress for everyone involved.

### Legal and Regulatory Considerations

When managing an estate, you must comply with UK probate laws and regulations to avoid delays, penalties, or legal action. Paying inheritance tax due to HMRC on time is important. You also need to submit accurate information to HMRC and notify relevant authorities, such as the Department for Work and Pensions.

To calculate the tax owed, you must understand inheritance tax rules. Familiarise yourself with exemptions, like spouse or charity exemptions, and thresholds, such as the nil-rate band. Knowing tax rates and reliefs, like business or agricultural property reliefs, also helps.

### FAQs

#### Do I Need a Solicitor to Apply for Probate?

You don't necessarily need a solicitor to apply for probate, but seeking professional help can simplify the process, especially for complex estates or contested wills.

#### How Long Does it Take to Get Probate Without a Solicitor?

The probate process typically takes 3-6 months without a solicitor, but timelines vary depending on the estate's complexity and your familiarity with the process.

#### What Documents Do I Need to Apply for Probate?

To apply for probate, you will need the original will and any codicils, a death certificate, proof of identity and address, and a completed probate application form (PA1). You may also need to submit inheritance tax forms and asset valuations and accounts. You can check out our guide for [probate without a will](https://lawhive.co.uk/wills-trust-probate/probate-without-a-will/) for more advice.

#### What Are the Costs of Applying for Probate Without a Solicitor?

The costs of applying for probate without a solicitor include the probate application fee, which ranges from £155 to £215, inheritance tax if applicable, court fees if contested, and valuation fees for assets.

#### What Happens If I Make a Mistake in My Probate Application?

If you make a mistake in your probate application, delays may occur, additional fees may be incurred, and HMRC or the Probate Registry may request corrections. In severe cases, the application may be rejected.

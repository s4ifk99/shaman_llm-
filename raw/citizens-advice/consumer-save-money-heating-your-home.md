#  Save money heating your home

###  If you have underfloor heating

Your underfloor heating might be:

  * heated using water from a central heating boiler or heat pump

  * heated using electricity

If your underfloor heating uses heated water from your boiler, there will be 2 metal rails with plastic tubes at the bottom called a 'manifold'. It’s usually in a cupboard.

![Manifold for underfloor heating system. There are 2 rows of metal rails. One has plastic caps on top. The other has gauges or meters on top. Plastic tubes come out of the bottom. Next to the rows of tubes and caps, there are several valves and dials to control the water coming in and out of the system.](//images.ctfassets.net/vms0u05139aw/7t27lvXmUwHnQgKpSaYQzv/01d3800bb250da2508a97ecfd3c559a3/manifold.jpeg)

You can follow the advice on this page to set the room temperature and heating times. Don't change any controls on the manifold, unless a heating engineer says you should.

If your system uses electricity, you won’t have a manifold. You can follow the advice on this page to set the room temperature and heating times.

###  If you have storage heaters

If you use storage heaters, you can [check how to use your storage heaters correctly](/consumer/energy/energy-supply/save-energy-at-home/using-storage-heaters-efficiently/).

###  If you have portable heaters

Try not to use portable heaters for long periods of time. They usually cost a lot more to run than turning on your central heating or using storage heaters.

If your central heating boiler isn’t working, you should fix or replace your boiler if you can. It’s much more expensive to use portable heaters on their own.

[Check what to do if you think your boiler is broken](/consumer/energy/energy-supply/save-energy-at-home/getting-your-broken-boiler-repaired-or-replaced/).

### Check the instructions for your central heating controls

Check if there are instructions written on your boiler. If you need to change the controls on your room thermostat, timer, programmer or radiator valves, the instructions might be written on them.

If you don’t have the instruction manual, search for it online - you’ll need to know who made your boiler or heating controls. You might also find videos that show how to use your controls.

If you’re not sure who made your heating controls, check your boiler or any separate controls for the manufacturer name and model number. There might be a sticker or manufacturing label. This might be underneath, on the side or inside a control panel or battery compartment.

### Set the best temperature for you and your home

Your room thermostat senses the temperature of the room it’s in. If the temperature reaches the level you've set, the room thermostat turns off your central heating for your whole home. If the temperature gets colder than the level you've set, it turns the heating on again for your whole home.

If your room thermostat is portable, don’t keep it near a radiator or behind curtains or furniture. Keep it in a central location like a hallway.

How to set the temperature depends on the device you have.

####  If you have a single device that is a timer or programmer and a room thermostat

You should set 2 temperatures for your home:

  * a comfortable temperature when you’re at home and awake

  * a lower temperature when you’re out or asleep - called a ‘setback temperature’

A temperature between 18 and 21 degrees Celsius is usually comfortable. If you have a health condition that might be made worse by the cold, ask your GP or a healthcare professional what temperature you should keep your home at.

If you're not sure what temperature will be comfortable, start with your room thermostat set at 21 degrees Celsius. Then try setting your room thermostat 1 degree Celsius lower and see if you still find the temperature comfortable. Repeat this process until you find your lowest comfortable temperature. This will save some money on your heating bill.

You should set the setback temperature to be 2 or 3 degrees Celsius lower than your comfortable temperature - but don't set it lower than 16 degrees Celsius. If your home is colder than 16 degrees Celsius, there’s a risk of your home becoming damp and you getting ill.

For example, you could set the temperature to 18 degrees Celsius at the times when you're home and 16 degrees Celsius at the times when you're out.

Setting higher temperatures won’t make your home heat up faster - it just means you'll use more energy to make your home hotter.

####  If you have a timer or programmer and a separate room thermostat

You should set a comfortable temperature for your home. A temperature between 18 and 21 degrees Celsius is usually comfortable. If you have a health condition that might be made worse by the cold, ask your GP or a healthcare professional what temperature you should keep your home at.

Don't set it lower than 16 degrees Celsius. If your home is colder than 16 degrees Celsius, there’s a risk of your home becoming damp and you getting ill.

If you're not sure what temperature will be comfortable, start with your room thermostat set at 21 degrees Celsius. Then try setting your room thermostat 1 degree Celsius lower and see if you still find the temperature comfortable. Repeat this process until you find your lowest comfortable temperature. This will save some money on your heating bill.

Setting higher temperatures won’t make your home heat up faster - it just means you'll use more energy to make your home hotter.

####  If you don’t have a room thermostat

It’s worth getting a room thermostat fitted if you can. You’ll be able to control the temperature more easily, and it might save you money on your heating bills.

You might be able to apply for help with the cost of installing heating controls.

[Check if you can get help paying for home improvements](/consumer/energy/energy-supply/save-energy-at-home/get-help-with-the-cost-of-energy-efficiency/).

If you can’t get help with the cost, ask a heating engineer or registered electrician to install the room thermostat. They should install it in a central location in your home, like a hallway.

Check if they’ll charge you to give you a quote for the work - and how much they'll charge.

If you have a gas boiler, you should use a Gas Safe registered engineer.

[Find an engineer on the Gas Safe Register website](https://www.gassaferegister.co.uk/find-an-engineer-or-check-the-register/).

If you use an electrician, make sure they are experienced with heating controls for boilers.

[Find a registered electrician on the Electrical Competent Person Scheme website](https://electricalcompetentperson.co.uk/).

### Set the times when your heating is on

Think about the times when you don’t need your heating to be on. For example if you’re out during the day, you could set the heating to be off or at the setback temperature until you’re due to come home.

It takes time for your home to warm up and cool down. If you’re not sure how long it takes, try turning the heating on about 30 minutes before you need it. You should turn it off 30 minutes before you’ll stop needing it. You might need to turn on your heating earlier if your home takes longer to reach a comfortable temperature.

If the weather is colder than usual, it might take longer for your home to reach a comfortable temperature. You might need to turn on your heating earlier while the weather is cold. When the weather gets warmer again, you’ll need to remember to change the timings back.

How to set the times when your heating is on depends on the device you have.

####  If you have a single device that is a timer or programmer and a room thermostat

You should set your device to heat your home to:

  * the comfortable temperature at times when you're going to be at home and awake

  * the setback temperature at times when you’re out or asleep

Make sure your heating follows the timings you’ve set. The setting might be labelled ‘auto’ or have a symbol of a clock. If you leave it on a different setting, it might ignore the timings you set.

You should also check that the clock is set to the current time so it turns the heating on when you want it to.

Example

Sam has a single device that is a programmer and a room thermostat.

On a normal day, Sam gets up at 6am. She leaves her home at 8am to go to work.

Sam sets her device to heat her home to:

  * 18 degrees Celsius starting at 5:30am - this means her home is warm when she wakes up

  * 16 degrees Celsius starting at 7:30am - this means her home isn't kept as warm while she's out

Sam gets home at 6pm. She goes to bed at 10pm.

Sam sets her device to heat her home to:

  * 18 degrees Celsius starting at 5:30pm - this means her home is warm when she's at home

  * 16 degrees Celsius starting at 9:30pm - this means her home isn't kept as warm while she's asleep

####  If you have a timer or programmer and a separate room thermostat

You should set the room thermostat to your comfortable temperature. Leave it set at that temperature.

Use the timer or programmer to:

  * turn the heating on at times when you're going to be at home and awake

  * turn the heating off when you’re out or asleep

Make sure your heating follows the timings you’ve set. The setting might be labelled ‘auto’ or have a symbol of a clock. If you leave it on a different setting, it might ignore the timings.

You should also check that the clock is set to the current time so it turns the heating on when you want it to.

### Check your radiator valves

Your radiator might have valves that let you set how warm it gets in each room.

Manual valves usually have a cap with no numbers:

![A manual valve attached to a heated towel rail. The cap on the valve doesn’t have any text or numbers on it.](//images.ctfassets.net/vms0u05139aw/2HOzrVLFwH2EqtspzuSNrn/edebf8c817350864d544a7c6ebd544f1/manual-radiator-valve.jpg)

Thermostatic radiator valves (TRVs) usually have a dial with numbers or symbols:

![A thermostatic radiator valve attached to a radiator pipe. The valve is set to show the number '3'.](//images.ctfassets.net/vms0u05139aw/thermostatic_radiator_valve.jpeg/256a23089ea8965964670be223e84c56/thermostatic_radiator_valve.jpeg)

Smart radiator valves (SRVs) can be controlled with an app on your smartphone. The SRV might have a small screen that displays the temperature, or buttons and dials to change it:

![A smart radiator valve attached to a radiator pipe. The valve has a small screen and a button. The top of the cap twists to control the temperature. The button is used for pairing with the smart phone app. The screen is blank.](//images.ctfassets.net/vms0u05139aw/4V3W6nTWyOI3acOvXA5fGF/4f8adb00d3caeaca654939bbd5bef1a9/srv-radiator-valve.jpg)

You might be able to change the setting on the valves to save energy and money on your heating bill.

Don’t change it to the lowest setting - this will turn off the radiator. Even if you don’t use the room much, you should still heat it a bit to stop damp and mould developing. It will also protect your pipes from freezing in cold weather.

How to change the setting on the valves depends on the type of valves your radiator has.

####  If you have manual valves

Manual valves work like a tap to let hot water into the radiator.

Turn the cap anti-clockwise to make the room warmer or clockwise to make it cooler.

Change the settings on the valve to be warmer in the rooms you use most. For example you might set it to be warmer in the living room, and cooler in a spare bedroom.

Don’t turn the cap all the way clockwise - this will turn off the radiator. If your room thermostat is in the same room, turning off the radiator means the room can’t reach the temperature on the thermostat. This might cause your heating to be on for too long.

####  If you have thermostatic radiator valves

TRVs work like a separate thermostat for each radiator. They sense the temperature in the room and automatically switch off the radiator when the room is warm enough.

The higher the number you set on the dial, the warmer the room will get. On a dial with numbers 0 to 5, the numbers usually mean:

  * 0: off

  * 1: 10 degrees Celsius

  * 2: 15 degrees Celsius

  * 3: 20 degrees Celsius

  * 4: 25 degrees Celsius

  * 5 or max: 30 degrees Celsius

Your TRV might also have a snowflake symbol. This is a low temperature which stops your pipes from freezing.

If your TRV has different numbers or symbols, check the user manual. If you can't find the user manual, check the manufacturer's website.

Set the dial to be warmer in the rooms you use most. For example you could set the dial to 3 in the living room, and 1 in a spare bedroom.

If there’s a cover on your radiator, the TRV will sense the temperature inside the radiator cover instead. This might be warmer than the number you set on the dial. If you can't remove the radiator cover, you might need to set the dial slightly higher so you can heat the room to the temperature you want.

If your room thermostat is in the same room as a TRV, make sure the TRV is set higher than the temperature on the room thermostat. If you set the TRV to a lower temperature than the room thermostat, the radiator won’t be able to heat the room to the temperature on the room thermostat. This means your heating might stay on for too long.

####  If you have smart radiator valves

SRVs work like a separate thermostat for each radiator. They sense the temperature in the room and automatically switch off the radiator when the room is warm enough.

You can normally control the temperature with a smartphone app. You might be able to set different temperatures in each room at different times of day.

Set the temperature to be warmer in the rooms you use most. For example you could set the temperature to 21 degrees Celsius in the living room, and 10 degrees Celsius in a spare bedroom.

If there’s a cover on your radiator, the SRV will sense the temperature inside the radiator cover instead. This might be warmer than the temperature you set. If you can't remove the radiator cover, you might need to set the temperature slightly higher so you can heat the room to the temperature you want.

If your room thermostat is in the same room as an SRV, make sure the SRV is set higher than the temperature on the thermostat. If you set the SRV to a lower temperature than the room thermostat, the radiator won’t be able to heat the room to the temperature on the room thermostat. This means your heating might stay on for too long.

## Check if you can reduce your boiler's flow temperature

The ‘flow temperature’ means the temperature of the water that your boiler uses to heat:

  * your radiators

  * your underfloor heating system

  * the water in your hot water cylinder, if you have one

### If you have a heat pump

Don’t change the flow temperature on your heat pump - unless your installer tells you it’s okay to do so.

If you think your heat pump costs too much to run or isn’t working correctly, contact your installer and ask them for advice. If you're renting, contact your landlord.

The flow temperature might be higher than it needs to be. You can save money if you set it to a lower temperature.

You might be able to change the flow temperature - it depends on the type of boiler you have.

### Check what type of boiler you have

If you have a boiler and a separate hot water tank or cylinder, you probably have a 'system boiler' or a 'regular boiler'.

Your hot water cylinder will probably be in a cupboard:

![A tall cylinder with a pipe out of the top and pipes on its side. A thermostat box is attached to it with a strap.](//images.ctfassets.net/vms0u05139aw/hot_water_cylinder.jpeg/afee3177eea5be5bef592aba78334476/hot_water_cylinder.jpeg)

If you don’t have a separate hot water cylinder, you probably have a combi boiler.

####  If you have a system or regular boiler

You can safely turn your boiler’s flow temperature down to a minimum of 65 degrees Celsius. Don’t set the flow temperature any lower - it must be at least 65 degrees Celsius to stop harmful bacteria growing inside your hot water cylinder.

Set the temperature using the controls on the boiler. The controls are usually labelled with a radiator symbol. They might be a dial with numbers or buttons and a screen.

Check the boiler’s user manual if you’re not sure. If you can't find the user manual, check the manufacturer's website.

If you don’t feel confident making the changes, you can ask a heating engineer to do it when you get your boiler serviced.

If you reduce the flow temperature, it might take longer to heat the water in the cylinder to a safe temperature. This means you should set your hot water controls to turn on earlier.

It might also take longer for your home to reach a comfortable temperature. You can set your timer or programmer to turn the heating on earlier. This means it has more time to heat up your home. For example, set it to turn on 1 hour before you need it instead of 30 minutes.

If your home still takes too long to heat up, you might need to increase the flow temperature again.

####  If you have a combi boiler

You can safely turn down your boiler's flow temperature. It won't change the temperature your tap water is heated to.

If you have radiators, try setting the flow temperature to 60 degrees Celsius.

If you have underfloor heating, try setting the flow temperature to 50 degrees Celsius.

If you want to save more energy, try setting the flow temperature 5 degrees Celsius lower and see if you still find the temperature comfortable. Repeat this process until you find your lowest comfortable temperature. This will save some money on your heating bill.

Set the temperature using the controls on the boiler. The controls are usually labelled with a radiator symbol. They might be a dial with numbers or buttons and a screen.

Check the boiler’s user manual if you’re not sure. If you can't find the user manual, check the manufacturer's website.

If you don’t feel confident making the changes, you can ask a heating engineer to do it when you get your boiler serviced.

If you reduce the flow temperature, it might take longer for your home to reach a comfortable temperature. You can set your timer or programmer to turn the heating on earlier. This means it has more time to heat up your home. For example, set it to turn on 1 hour before you need it instead of 30 minutes.

If your home still takes too long to heat up, you might need to increase the flow temperature again.

## Setting your hot water controls

If you have central heating, your boiler heats the water that comes out of your taps.

You might be able to save energy using your hot water controls. You might be able to:

  * change when the water is heated

  * lower the temperature your water is heated to

### If you have a heat pump

Don’t change the programme times or water temperature on your heat pump - unless your installer tells you it’s okay to do so.

If you think your heat pump costs too much to run or isn’t working correctly, contact your installer and ask them for advice. If you're renting, contact your landlord.

What you can change depends on the type of boiler you have.

If you heat your hot water with immersion heaters or use electric showers, they don’t use your boiler to heat the water - they use electricity instead. [Check how to save money using electrical appliances](/consumer/energy/energy-supply/save-energy-at-home/save-money-using-your-electrical-appliances/).

### Check what type of boiler you have

If you have a boiler and a separate hot water tank or cylinder, you probably have a ‘system boiler’ or a 'regular boiler'.

Your hot water cylinder will probably be in a cupboard:

![A tall cylinder with a pipe out of the top and pipes on its side. A thermostat box is attached to it with a strap.](//images.ctfassets.net/vms0u05139aw/hot_water_cylinder.jpeg/afee3177eea5be5bef592aba78334476/hot_water_cylinder.jpeg)

If you don’t have a separate hot water cylinder, you probably have a combi boiler.

### Check if you can change when your water is heated

You might be able to change when your water is heated - it depends on the type of boiler you have.

####  If you have a system or regular boiler

A system or regular boiler heats the hot water cylinder. The cylinder slowly heats your tap water and keeps it hot until you need it.

You can set the times when your water is heated using a timer or programmer.

Your hot water timer or programmer might be:

  * the same device that controls your heating

  * a separate device from your heating timer or programmer

You might need to set your timer or programmer to heat your hot water more than once per day - for example if you usually take a shower in the morning and someone else in your household takes a bath in the evening.

It takes time for the cylinder to heat up enough hot water. You'll probably need to start heating your water 1 or 2 hours before you need it, but it depends on the size of your cylinder and how well insulated it is.

It’s safe to make changes yourself. If you don’t feel confident making the changes, you can ask a heating engineer to do it when you get your boiler serviced.

Don’t leave your hot water on all the time. It will use more energy and your heating bills will be higher.

Example

Maryam gets up at 7am. She has a shower and uses the rest of the water during the day for washing up.

Maryam’s sister usually has a shower at 9pm.

Maryam sets her programmer to:

  * heat her hot water at 6am, and turn off at 7am

  * heat her hot water at 8pm, and turn off at 9pm

##### If you also have an immersion heater

Your hot water cylinder might also have an immersion heater for heating extra hot water when you need it.

An immersion heater uses electricity to heat your water. It looks like a round plastic cap coming out of the cylinder with an electrical cable attached to it.

Immersion heaters can be very expensive to run. You should use your boiler to heat most of the water you use, and avoid using the immersion heater often.

If you run out of hot water and can’t wait for your boiler to heat up the cylinder, you can set the immersion heater to turn on for a short time. For example, you could turn it on for 30 minutes.

Your immersion heater might have its own timer with a ‘boost’ button or a switch to turn it on or off. If it has a switch, remember to switch it off when you have enough hot water.

###### If your central heating boiler isn’t working

It’s much more expensive to use your immersion heater on its own if your boiler isn’t working. You should fix or replace your boiler if you can.

[Check what to do if you think your boiler is broken](/consumer/energy/energy-supply/save-energy-at-home/getting-your-broken-boiler-repaired-or-replaced/).

####  If you have a combi boiler

A combi boiler heats up tap water when you turn the hot tap on.

Your boiler might have a 'pre-heat' setting. This keeps the boiler warm so your water gets hot more quickly when you turn on the hot tap.

You can save energy by switching off the boiler’s ‘pre-heat’ setting or switching it to ‘eco’. However it will take longer for your tap water to get hot, especially if your heating is off.

It’s safe to make changes yourself. If you don’t feel confident making the changes, you can ask a heating engineer to do it when you get your boiler serviced.

While you wait for the tap water to get hot, you can collect the cooler water in a jug or watering can by the sink. You can use it for something else, for example watering plants.

### Check if you can change the temperature of your water

Your tap water might be hotter than it needs to be.

You might be able to change the temperature of the water - it depends on the type of boiler you have.

####  If you have a system or regular boiler

There should be a thermostat to control the temperature of your hot water. It’s usually attached to the hot water cylinder.

You can safely turn down the temperature to a minimum of 60 degrees Celsius. Don’t set it any lower - your tap water must be heated to at least 60 degrees Celsius to stop harmful bacteria growing in your hot water cylinder.

####  If you have a combi boiler

You can set the temperature using the controls on the boiler. The controls are usually labelled with a tap symbol. They might be a dial with numbers or buttons and a screen.

Check the user manual for your boiler if you’re not sure. If you can't find the user manual, check the manufacturer's website.

You could try setting the temperature to 50 degrees Celsius to start with. You can set it lower to save more energy and money on your heating bills, but the water might not feel hot enough.

## Insulate your hot water cylinder

Insulation will help your cylinder heat the water more quickly and keep it hot for longer.

You can buy a hot water cylinder jacket from a DIY shop or order it online. It’s cheap and you can install it yourself. In a year, you’ll probably save more money on your heating bills than the cost of the jacket.

If you already have foam insulation on your cylinder, you can add a jacket on top to save more energy and money on your heating bills.

If you already have a jacket fitted on your cylinder, check it’s at least 80mm thick. If it isn’t, consider buying a new one. You’ll save more energy and money on your heating bills.

## Use your radiators effectively

If you have radiators, you should make sure you’re using them effectively. You should:

  * remove trapped air from your radiators - this is known as ‘bleeding’ radiators

  * keep the area around radiators clear

You might also want to install a radiator reflector if your home is not well insulated.

### Remove trapped air from your radiators

If there is trapped air in your radiators, they will not heat your room as much as they should. You can remove the trapped air by bleeding the radiators.

It’s best to bleed your radiators once every year. You might want to do it before you put your heating on when the weather gets colder.

Before you start, check you know how to repressurise your boiler. You might need to do this after you bleed your radiators - check your boiler's instructions if you’re not sure how.

Find the bleed valve - it’s usually on one side of the radiator, at the top. It usually looks like a small slotted or square shaped valve. Depending on the shape, you’ll need to use a flathead screwdriver or a radiator key. If you don’t have a radiator key, you can buy one online or from a DIY shop.

Make sure your heating is off. Start with the radiator furthest away from the boiler. You should follow these steps to bleed each radiator:

  1. Check the radiator is cool.

  2. Put a towel under or around the bleed valve to catch any water that drips out.

  3. Insert the screwdriver or radiator key into the bleed valve. Turn it slowly anticlockwise. You should hear a hissing sound as air escapes. If the valve is very stiff, don’t force it.

  4. When water starts to come out, close the valve by turning the key clockwise.

  5. Check the valve is closed properly - there should be no water leaking out. If you can’t close it any further and the valve is leaking, contact a plumber. If you have plumber's tape, you can use this to stop the leak while you wait for the plumber.

When you’ve bled all your radiators, check if you need to repressurise your boiler.

When you’re ready, turn on the heating and check each radiator gets warm.

### Don’t cover radiators

Radiators heat the air around the radiator. This warm air heats the room. Keep the space around your radiators clear so that warm air can flow. Your room will warm up quicker and you’ll use less energy heating your home.

Move furniture away from the radiator - try to move it at least 1 radiator’s width away.

If there’s a cover on your radiator, remove it if you can.

If you have curtains, make sure they don’t hang over the top or in front of your radiator. If they do, it's worth swapping them for shorter curtains.

### Use a radiator reflector

Radiator reflectors are heat-reflective panels that you put behind your radiators. They can help reflect a small amount of heat back into the room. They’re usually made of a special type of foil.

They work best if you use them on external walls. They’re not worth installing if your home is well-insulated, or if your radiators are on walls between rooms.

You can usually install a radiator reflector yourself and it’s easy to remove.

You can buy a radiator reflector kit or make your own from foil insulation. Check the packaging to make sure it can be used as a ‘radiant barrier’ or reflects radiant heat.

## Insulate central heating and hot water pipes

Insulating central heating and hot water pipes might reduce the amount of lost heat, which helps you save energy and money on your heating bills.

You don’t need to insulate every pipe - ask a plumber or heating engineer if any of your pipes are worth insulating. Insulation works best if your pipes go through a colder place like a loft or garage.

You can buy insulation tubes and install them yourself, or ask a plumber or heating engineer to install insulation.

## Keep heat in your home

There might be free or low cost ways you can keep heat in your home.

This can save you money because you won’t need your heating on as much.

### Close your curtains and blinds at night and open them during the day

Close your curtains and blinds when it gets dark. This helps keep the heat in at night.

Keep your curtains or blinds open when it's light. This lets the sun warm the room.

### Add secondary glazing to your windows

Secondary glazing is an extra layer you can add to your windows to reduce the amount of heat that escapes through them.

If you have single-glazed or older double-glazed windows, it might be cheaper to add secondary glazing than to completely replace the windows.

It’s also worth considering adding secondary glazing if you can’t replace your windows. For example, this might be because your home is a listed building or you live in an area with extra planning restrictions.

You could add:

  * secondary glazing film - a clear, thin material

  * plastic secondary glazing sheets - a clear, thicker plastic

  * a second window - another frame and pane fitted inside the existing windows

Search online for ‘secondary glazing film’ or ‘secondary glazing kits’ to find places you can buy it.

You can usually add secondary glazing film or sheets yourself. It’s easy to remove. Make sure you remove it carefully to avoid damage to the window frame. You can usually reuse the sheets, but you can’t reuse the film. If you use your window as a fire escape or for ventilation, it’s better to use sheets.

A second frame might be more efficient at stopping heat escaping from your windows than secondary glazing kits or film. It's more expensive and you’ll usually need to find a glazing installer to add it to your window.

[Check how to find an installer](/consumer/energy/energy-supply/save-energy-at-home/find-an-installer-for-energy-efficiency-home-improvements/).

## Find ways to block unwanted draughts

You can keep heat in your home by blocking unwanted gaps and cracks that let warm air escape. This is known as ‘draught proofing’.

You need to let damp air escape to avoid problems with condensation and mould. This is called ‘ventilation’.

### If you have problems with condensation or mould

If your home has mould that keeps growing, or a lot of condensation, you should contact someone to fix the problem. It's important to deal with those problems first, as draught proofing could make them worse.

If you rent your home, your landlord is usually responsible for fixing damp. [Check what to do if you have damp or mould in your rented home](/housing/repairs-and-housing/repairs-and-housing-conditions/whos-responsible-for-repairs/repairs-damp/).

If you own your home, you might need advice from a damp specialist. [Check how to find a damp specialist on the Property Care Association website](https://www.property-care.org/find-a-specialist/).

### How you can find and block draughts

You’re most likely to find draughts around windows and doors. You should also check for draughts from:

  * a chimney you don’t use

  * floorboards and skirting boards

  * the loft hatch

  * openings in walls for pipes

It’s best to look for draughts on a cool or windy day, when they’re more noticeable. You might:

  * feel cold air where heat is escaping

  * see objects moving, like curtains or blinds

  * hear sounds like whistling or rattling

If you find a draught, you might be able to block it. You could try self-adhesive strips, rubber seals or draught excluders to cover the gap. You can also use DIY products, like fillers, sealants or expanding foam.

### Make sure some air still flows through your home

Good ventilation will help you avoid problems with condensation and mould. It will also make the air in your home fresher and safer to breathe, and only lose a small amount of heat.

You might have features in your home to help you keep it ventilated. For example, you might have:

  * extractor fans - these are usually in bathrooms or kitchens

  * small vents on windows or doors called trickle vents

  * openings in walls called vents or air bricks

####  Ventilating your kitchen and bathroom

When you’re cooking, bathing or showering, you should always:

  * use the extractor fan, if you have one

  * open a window, if you can

  * keep the bathroom or kitchen door closed

All these things will help damp air escape your home.

It’s important to keep extractor fans clean so they work properly. [Check how to clean your bathroom extractor fan on the Electrical Safety First website](https://www.electricalsafetyfirst.org.uk/guidance/safety-around-the-home/extractor-fan-safety/).

If your bathroom or kitchen doesn’t have an extractor fan, you might want to get one installed.

If you decide to get an extractor fan installed, [read our advice about finding an installer](/consumer/energy/energy-supply/save-energy-at-home/find-an-installer-for-energy-efficiency-home-improvements/).

####  Ventilating your other rooms

If you have trickle vents, keep them open and make sure the area around openings is clear.

If you don’t have vents in a room, you can ventilate your home by opening windows for a few minutes every day. This is particularly important if you notice condensation in a room, for example on the inside of your bedroom window in the morning.

It’s worth getting trickle vents installed on your windows if your room doesn’t already have them.

If you plan to make any changes to your home, [read our advice about finding an installer](/consumer/energy/energy-supply/save-energy-at-home/find-an-installer-for-energy-efficiency-home-improvements/).

You should also keep furniture a few inches away from walls - this lets air flow behind furniture.

If you have gaps underneath your internal doors, you shouldn’t block them. They are there to let air flow between rooms.

If you do laundry at home, hang your laundry in a room that’s well ventilated. For example, a kitchen, bathroom or utility room. If you have access to outdoor space and the ground is dry, you could hang your laundry outside.

## Use heated throws or blankets

You can use heated throws or blankets to keep yourself warm without turning up your heating. They use electricity, but only cost around 3p each hour to run.

You should check that any heated throw or blanket you have is safe to use. Look at the label and make sure it has a safety mark.

It might have a ‘UKCA’ safety mark:

![](//images.ctfassets.net/vms0u05139aw/ukca_mark.jpg/61788f823cc652788ad36a1cbf323902/ukca_mark.jpg)

Or it might have a ‘CE’ safety mark:

![A CE safety mark. It's has the letters 'C E' in a row.](//images.ctfassets.net/vms0u05139aw/ce_mark.jpg/ff3202a3821c9e9d41eb5caf4b7939cf/ce_mark.jpg)

If your throw or blanket doesn’t have either of these marks, don’t use it.

[Check how to use your electric blanket safely on the Electrical Safety First website](https://www.electricalsafetyfirst.org.uk/guidance/product-safety/electric-blankets/).

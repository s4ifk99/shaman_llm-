#  Your energy supplier has gone bust

###  If you're on the Priority Services Register

If you were on your old supplier’s Priority Services Register, ask your new supplier if you’re still on it.

You can ask to be added to the new supplier’s Priority Services Register if this has not happened automatically. If you’re on the Priority Services Register you’ll get extra help or services free of charge. Read our advice on [getting extra support from your energy supplier](/consumer/energy/energy-supply/get-help-paying-your-bills/getting-extra-support-from-your-energy-supplier/).

###  If you have a prepayment meter

If you’re a prepayment customer, you should only top up in small amounts until your new account is set up. It’s best to pay only what you need to get through a few days.

This is because if you have a smart prepayment meter, there’s a chance that your credit could be wiped when your account is moved. You’ll get your money back if this happens, but this can take a long time.

If you think there’s a fault with your meter you should contact your new supplier.

Your new supplier should:

  * let you know how to top up your meter

  * provide a new prepayment meter if necessary - they shouldn’t charge you for this

Check where your nearest top-up point is - it might not be the one you’ve been using. Find out more about [topping up your prepayment meter](/consumer/energy/energy-supply/problems-with-your-energy-supply/problems-getting-to-or-topping-up-your-prepayment-meter/).

If you can’t top up, [contact our consumer service](/about-us/contact-us/contact-us/consumer-service/).

Your new supplier should tell you how much emergency credit you can get. It might be different from your previous supplier.

Your new tariff might be more expensive than the old one. You might be able to find a cheaper tariff if you [switch to a different energy supplier](/consumer/energy/energy-supply/get-a-better-energy-deal/switching-energy-supplier/). You won’t have to pay an exit fee.

###  If you get the Warm Home Discount

If you get the Warm Home Discount, your new supplier will tell you if you can still get it. If you can’t get it with your new supplier, you can switch to a different supplier. [Check which suppliers are part of the Warm Home Discount scheme on the GOV.UK website](https://www.gov.uk/the-warm-home-discount-scheme/energy-suppliers).

## If you asked to switch from a supplier before it went bust

You’ll still move to the new supplier you chose. You won’t need to do anything.

The supplier that’s taken over your old supplier should pay you back any money you’re owed when you move.

If you pay by direct debit, you don’t need to cancel it straight away. Your direct debit details will move to your new supplier and your old direct debit will end.

If you want to cancel your direct debit, wait until your new account is set up.

## If you were switching to a supplier when it went bust

Wait to hear from the supplier that’s taken over the supplier that went bust. They should tell you whether:

  * they'll take over your account

  * you'll stay with the supplier you were switching away from

If your switch went through and you've already paid in advance for your energy, your money is protected. The supplier that's taken over the supplier that went bust should pay you back any money you're owed.

## If you recently switched to a new supplier

If your old supplier went bust and still owes you money, your money is protected. The supplier that's taken over the supplier that went bust should pay you back any money you're owed.

If the supplier doesn’t pay you, you should make a complaint. [Check how to complain to your energy supplier](/consumer/energy/energy-supply/complain-about-an-energy-company/complain-to-your-energy-supplier/).

## If you complained to your old supplier and it hasn’t been resolved

You should raise the complaint again with your new supplier. It might take a while for your information to be moved from your old supplier to your new supplier. This means your new supplier might not be able to look into your complaint straight away.

If you’ve complained to the energy Ombudsman, they’ll contact you about their decision.

## If you have problems with your meter

You should contact your new supplier to report any problems. You can get their contact details from their website.

It might take a while for your information to be moved from your old supplier to your new supplier. This means your new supplier might not be able to look into your problem and get back to you straight away.

## If you were in debt to your old supplier

If you were paying a debt to your old supplier you’ll still have to pay this back.

Wait for your new supplier to contact you. If they’re taking on your debt, they’ll let you know.

If the new supplier isn’t taking on your debt, you’ll have to pay an administrator instead - this is an organisation that takes over a company that goes bust.

### If your new supplier takes on your debt

Your new supplier will usually contact you to arrange a payment plan. If you’re struggling to pay, they have to help you find a way to pay.

You should try to negotiate a deal that works for both of you. Find out [what to do if you’re struggling to pay your energy bills](/consumer/energy/energy-supply/get-help-paying-your-bills/struggling-to-pay-your-energy-bills/) if you need help with this.

### If you have to pay an administrator

The administrator will collect debts for your old supplier. You might get a bill from the administrator instead of your old supplier.

If you were using a prepayment meter to pay off your debt, you might not be able to use the meter to pay the administrator. They’ll tell you how to pay.

You might be asked to pay all of your debt back at once. This is because administrators don’t have to follow the same payment rules as energy companies.

You should still try to negotiate a payment plan with the administrator. They should consider this even if they do not agree to it. Speak to your new supplier if your debt means you’re struggling to pay your energy bills.

## If you get a bill you didn’t expect

Check the bill against your old bills and statements. If you’re not able to pay, contact the company that sent you the bill to discuss your options.

The bill might be from:

  * your new energy supplier

  * an 'administrator' or 'insolvency practitioner'

  * a 'debt purchaser'

An administrator or insolvency practitioner is an organisation that takes over a company that goes bust.

A debt purchaser is a company that buys the debt owed to your old supplier. This means the money is now owed to them.

When a debt purchaser buys your debt, you should get letters explaining the situation from both them and the administrator or new supplier. The letters should explain they have the legal right to collect the money owed.

If you’re worried the bill might be a scam, contact your new supplier. They’ll be able to tell you if it’s genuine.

### If you get a bill that includes energy you used over a year ago

You might not have to pay for all of it. Under ‘back billing’ rules, a company can’t charge for energy you used more than 12 months ago.

The back billing rules apply to all the companies that might send you an energy bill.

The back billing rules don’t apply if:

  * you were sent a bill before the year passed and you didn’t pay

  * you‘ve acted unreasonably, for example not allowing your supplier to access your meter

If you think a company is charging you for energy used over a year ago, there are things you can do.

####  If your bill is from your new supplier

You should complain to the supplier. You'll find their contact details on their website.

Tell them that you’re protected by the back billing rules and should only be charged for 1 year's energy use. If they say you’ve been billed before and you don’t agree, ask to see evidence.

You can use our [back billing example letter](/consumer/template-letters/letters/energy-letters/complain-to-a-supplier-about-back-billing/).

[Check how to contact your energy supplier and what to do if they won’t help](/consumer/energy/energy-supply/complain-about-an-energy-company/complain-to-your-energy-supplier/).

####  If your bill is from the administrator or insolvency practitioner

You should complain to the administrator. You'll find their contact details on their website.

Tell them that you’re protected by the back billing rules and should only be charged for 1 year's energy use. If they say you’ve been billed before and you don’t agree, ask to see evidence.

If the administrator says that the back billing rules don't apply to them, tell them that:

  * the rules were part of your contract with your old supplier - it’s part of the Standard Licence Conditions

  * they can’t ask for any payment that you didn’t owe to the old supplier because of back billing rules

If the administrator doesn’t help you, your next step is to [complain to their professional body by filling in the complaints form on GOV.UK](https://www.gov.uk/complain-about-insolvency-practitioner).

If you need any support with your complaint, [talk to an adviser](/about-us/contact-us/contact-us/contact-us/).

####  If your bill is from a debt purchaser

You should complain to the debt purchaser. You'll find their contact details on their website.

Tell them that you’re protected by the back billing rules and should only be charged for 1 year's energy use. If they say you’ve been billed before and you don’t agree, ask to see evidence.

If the debt purchaser says that the back billing rules don't apply to them, tell them that:

  * the rules were part of your contract with your old supplier - it’s part of the Standard Licence Conditions

  * the debt purchaser has to follow the rules of your contract with your old supplier

If the debt purchaser won’t help you, find out if they belong to the Credit Services Association (CSA). You can search for the company on the CSA website - use both the 'Company / Employer' and 'Trading style' boxes to search. [Search the CSA Member Directory on their website](https://www.csa-uk.com/search/custom.asp?id=4661).

If they’re a member, [complain to the CSA on their website](https://www.csa-uk.com/page/complaints). Explain that you think the debt purchaser has broken the back billing rules.

If the debt purchaser isn’t a member of the CSA or you need any extra support, [talk to a debt adviser](/about-us/contact-us/contact-us/contact-us/).

## If a direct debit payment accidentally goes to an old supplier

Your new supplier is responsible for refunding any money you’re owed - including any accidental direct debit payments to the old supplier.

If you’re having a problem sorting this out you can complain to your new supplier, and to your bank.

## If you’re worried about paying for your energy

You might be able to get help from the government and your energy supplier. [Check what grants or benefits you could get to help you pay your energy bills](/consumer/energy/energy-supply/get-help-paying-your-bills/grants-and-benefits-to-help-you-pay-your-energy-bills/).

If you’re struggling to pay your bills, [check what extra help you can get](/debt-and-money/get-help-with-the-cost-of-living/).

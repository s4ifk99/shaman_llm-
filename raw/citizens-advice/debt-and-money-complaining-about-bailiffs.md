#  Complaining about bailiffs

###  You haven’t paid a parking ticket

Check what kind of parking ticket you have. It could be a ‘penalty charge notice’ from the council or a ‘parking charge notice’ from a company.

#### If you have a penalty charge notice

If you want to complain about getting the penalty charge notice rather than about the bailiffs, check if you can [appeal the penalty charge notice](/law-and-courts/parking-tickets/appealing-parking-tickets/appealing-a-parking-ticket/).

To complain about the bailiffs, follow your council's complaints process. Most councils have an online form you can fill in to send a complaint. You can [find your council's website on GOV.UK](https://www.gov.uk/find-local-council).

If you can’t find your council’s complaints process, write to their postal address. This should be in the contact section of their website. Address your letter to the council’s legal department.

#### If you have a parking charge notice

If you want to complain about getting the parking charge notice rather than about the bailiffs, check if you can [appeal the parking charge notice](/law-and-courts/parking-tickets/appealing-parking-tickets/appealing-a-parking-ticket/).

The company that fined you will have had to get a county court judgement to send bailiffs after you. To complain about the bailiffs, you’ll need to write to the court that made the judgement. [Use the court finder on GOV.UK](https://www.gov.uk/find-court-tribunal) to get the court’s email or postal address. Address your complaint to the bailiff manager.

###  You owe a magistrate’s court fine

[Use the court finder on GOV.UK](https://www.gov.uk/find-court-tribunal) to get the court’s email or postal address. Then address your email or letter to the court clerk.

###  You owe money for a county court judgment

[Use the court finder on GOV.UK](https://www.gov.uk/find-court-tribunal) to get the court’s email or postal address. Then address your email or letter to the court’s bailiff manager.

###  You owe income tax, VAT or National Insurance

[Complain to HM Revenue and Customs](https://www.gov.uk/government/organisations/hm-revenue-customs/contact/complain-about-hmrc) on their website. It’s best to use their online form so it reaches them instantly and you can keep a record of what you’ve sent. You can complain by phone or letter if you prefer.

###  You owe child support

Check if you owe your debt to the Child Maintenance Service. Letters and other documents from the bailiffs should tell you this.

For the Child Maintenance Service, or if you’re not sure, write to:

Child Maintenance Service (CMS)

PO Box 249

Mitcheldean

GL17 1AJ

Phone: 0800 171 2345

Text relay: 0345 266 8795

For the Child Support Agency, [find your nearest office on the DWP’s website](https://www.gov.uk/child-maintenance-service/contact).

## How to write your complaint to the creditor

You'll need to send an email or letter to your creditor. [Find out how to write a letter complaining about bailiffs](/debt-and-money/action-your-creditor-can-take/bailiffs/how-bailiffs-should-treat-you/writing-a-complaint-letter-about-bailiffs/) \- including what to ask the creditor to do.

## Send your complaint to the bailiffs

Make sure you send your complaint to the bailiffs as well as to your creditor. Look on the bailiff’s website for their complaints process. You can also ask a bailiff how to complain if they visit.

If complaining to your creditor doesn’t help and you want to take more action against the bailiffs, you’ll need to show you’ve used the bailiff’s complaints process. This means you must address the complaint to the bailiffs, and tell them you want to make a complaint about them.

### If the bailiff doesn't have a complaints process

If bailiffs won’t tell you how to complain, add this to your complaint to your creditor. Say that this is a breach of the national standards all bailiffs should follow. Ask your creditor to take back the debt and end the bailiff action.

If the bailiff is accredited by the Enforcement Conduct Board (ECB), you can also say it’s a breach of the ECB rules. Most bailiffs are accredited by the ECB - you can [check if a bailiff is accredited on the ECB website](https://enforcementconductboard.org/directory/).

## Complaining about the creditor's response

Some creditors will have an ombudsman. This is a separate organisation that makes sure they’re behaving properly. You can complain to an ombudsman about bailiffs collecting debts for:

  * your local council

  * a magistrate’s court

  * HM Revenue and Customs (HMRC)

  * the Department for Work and Pensions (DWP)

  * an energy, internet or phone company

Complaining to an ombudsman is free, but you need to have complained to your creditor first.

###  If you owe money to your council

You can [complain to the Local Government and Social Care Ombudsman](http://www.lgo.org.uk/make-a-complaint). Your complaint should be about the council that you sent your original complaint to. Explain what the bailiffs have done wrong but focus on your council’s response to your original complaint.

You’ll need to wait until you hear back from the council before you complain to the ombudsman.

###  If you owe money to a magistrate’s court, HMRC or the DWP

You’ll need to complain to the Parliamentary and Health Services Ombudsman. To do this you’ll need to fill in the ombudsman’s [complaints form for UK government services](https://www.ombudsman.org.uk/making-complaint/complain-us-getting-started/complaint-forms) and ask your MP to send it for you.

Find your MP’s contact details by [entering your postcode on the Parliament website](http://www.parliament.uk/mps-lords-and-offices/mps/). Contact them and say:

  * what the bailiffs have done wrong

  * why the HMRC, DWP or court response hasn’t helped

  * you’d like to refer HMRC, the DWP or the court to the Parliamentary and Health Services Ombudsman

  * that you’ve included the ombudsman’s complaints form and you’d like them to fill in section 5

###  If you owe money to an energy company

You can [complain to the energy ombudsman](/consumer/energy/energy-supply/complain-about-an-energy-company/complain-to-the-energy-ombudsman/) about bailiffs sent by an energy company. You’ll need to have complained directly to the energy company first.

###  If you owe money to an internet or phone company

You can [complain to the communications ombudsman](/consumer/get-more-help/how-to-use-an-ombudsman-in-england/) about bailiffs sent by an internet or phone company. You’ll need to have complained directly to the company first.

## Complaining about the bailiff's response

You might be able to take your complaint further. This will depend on whether the bailiff is accredited by the Enforcement Conduct Board (ECB).

Most bailiffs are accredited by the ECB - you can [check if a bailiff is accredited on the ECB website](https://enforcementconductboard.org/directory/).

### If the bailiff is accredited by the ECB

The ECB can only look into complaints about bailiff activity that happened on or after 1 January 2025.

Complaining to the ECB is free. If you haven't complained to the bailiffs yet, you can't complain to the ECB.

You must send your complaint to the ECB by whichever date is later:

  * within 3 months of first knowing you have a complaint

  * within 1 month of getting a final response from the bailiffs’ complaints process

If you haven’t heard from the bailiffs after 20 days of sending your complaint to them, you can also complain to the ECB.

[Check how to make a complaint online on the ECB website](https://enforcementconductboard.org/making-a-complaint/) \- you can also download a complaints form which you can send to the ECB by email or post.

### If the bailiff isn’t accredited with the ECB

You can complain to a bailiff trade body.

You can only complain to a trade body once you’ve heard back from the bailiff company you sent a copy of your complaint to. You can also complain to a trade body if you haven’t heard anything from the bailiffs after 28 days.

You can [complain to the Civil Enforcement Association](http://www.civea.co.uk/complaints/) if your debt is for magistrates' court fines or child support payments.

You can complain to the High Court Enforcement Officers’ Association (HCEOA) if you’re dealing with high court enforcement officers. Bailiffs have to say if they’re high court enforcement officers - it will be on any letters they’ve sent you, and on the ID they have to show you if they visit you.

To complain to the High Court Enforcement Officers' Association, email your complaint to [complaints@hceoa.org.uk](mailto:complaints@hceoa.org.uk). You can also [fill in the form on the HCEOA website](https://www.hceoa.org.uk/contact-us/make-a-complaint).

You can also complain by calling 0844 824 4575 - but it’s better to email or fill in the online form if you can. By emailing you can make your complaint more detailed and keep a record of what you sent.

## Take legal action against a bailiff

If a bailiff has broken the rules, you can apply to court to either:

  * get compensation

  * have a bailiff’s certificate taken away - this means they can’t work as a bailiff anymore

### Get compensation

You can apply for compensation if a bailiff has broken the rules - for example if they've taken things that don’t belong to you.

The court can order the bailiff to return what they’ve taken from you, or pay you the value of your belongings if the bailiff has sold them.

The court can order the bailiff to pay you for:

  * any money you've lost because of the bailiff’s actions - for example having to get taxis to work because the bailiff took your car

  * hurt or distress you've suffered because of the bailiff’s actions - this is called 'injury to feelings'

  * any fees you had to pay the bailiff

If you don’t win the court case, you might have to pay the court costs - this can be expensive. Going to court can also be stressful and time consuming.

If you’re considering court action against bailiffs, you should [talk to an adviser](/about-us/contact-us/contact-us/contact-us/). An adviser can discuss the options with you.

If you do decide to take court action, it’s best to get legal help. [Check if you can get free or affordable legal advice](/law-and-courts/legal-system/finding-free-or-affordable-legal-help/).

Get a bailiff’s certificate taken away

If a bailiff has behaved so badly you don’t think they should be allowed to be a bailiff any more, it can be worth applying to court to have their certificate taken away. This will stop them working as a bailiff - they’ll stop contacting you and you won’t have to pay their fees.

If you’re considering court action against bailiffs, you should [talk to an adviser](/about-us/contact-us/contact-us/contact-us/). An adviser can discuss the options with you.

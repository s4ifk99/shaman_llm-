#  Complaining about an energy efficiency home improvement

###  If your private landlord arranged the work

Try to talk to your landlord or letting agent informally first - explain the situation and share any evidence you have such as paperwork or photographs.

It’s usually your landlord’s responsibility to do repairs if the energy efficiency home improvement either:

  * isn’t working properly

  * causes another problem in your home

For example if your heating system isn’t heating your home enough and causes damp and mould, your landlord should do repairs.

[Check if your landlord has to do repairs](/housing/repairs-and-housing/repairs-and-housing-conditions/whos-responsible-for-repairs/check-if-your-landlord-has-to-do-repairs/).

You can complain to your landlord, if for example:

  * you aren’t happy with their response when you ask for a repair

  * the energy efficiency home improvement works, but you aren’t happy with it - for example if you’ve noticed your energy bills are higher after it was installed

You can find out about your options, including how to complain - [check how to complain if you're renting privately](/housing/repairs-and-housing/repairs-and-housing-conditions/getting-your-landlord-to-do-repairs/getting-repairs-done-if-youre-renting-privately-ew/getting-repairs-done-if-youre-renting-privately/).

If you’re worried about your landlord evicting you, [talk to an adviser](/about-us/contact-us/).

###  If your shared ownership landlord arranged the work

If your home is shared ownership, your social landlord might have arranged the home improvement work.

If you can, speak with your neighbours and check if they’ve had the same work done. They might have a similar problem with the work and already complained.

You should complain to your landlord using their complaints process. You should clearly explain the problem in your complaint. Include copies of any evidence you have, for example, photographs or paperwork.

If you’re not sure how to complain, contact The Leasehold Advisory Service (LEASE) for free advice.

[Check the leaseholder advice or book a telephone appointment on the LEASE website](https://www.lease-advice.org/#).

You can also book a telephone appointment by calling:

Telephone: 0207 832 2500

Monday to Friday, 9am to 4:15pm

###  If your freeholder or management company arranged the work

You'll need to contact your freeholder or management company to ask them for details of their complaints process. If you need to find their contact details, check the paperwork you got from your solicitor when you bought your home.

If your neighbours have had the same work done, check to see if they’re having any problems - they might have already complained.

You should clearly explain the problem in your complaint. Include copies of any evidence you have, for example, photographs or paperwork.

#### If you’ve complained and can’t come to an agreement

If you don’t get a reply to your complaint or you’re not happy with your freeholder or management company’s response, contact The Leasehold Advisory Service (LEASE) for free advice.

[Check the leaseholder advice or book a telephone appointment on the LEASE website](https://www.lease-advice.org/#).

You can also book a telephone appointment by calling:

Telephone: 0207 832 2500

Monday to Friday, 9am to 4:15pm

## If you don’t know who arranged the work

You might not know who arranged the work because the work was done before you moved into your home.

If you have a landlord or management company, check if they arranged the work.

### Check who the installer was if you own your home

You might have been given papers by your solicitor, or they might have been left in the home when you moved in.

Check any paperwork for:

  * the name of the installer who did the work - this should be on any quotes, invoices or receipts

  * a warranty or guarantee for the work

  * any dates when the work was carried out

If you can find the name of the installer, try contacting them for help. They might be able to come and check the work.

If you can find a guarantee or warranty but it’s registered to the previous owner, you might be able to claim under it. [Contact the Citizens Advice consumer service](/consumer/get-more-help/if-you-need-more-help-about-a-consumer-issue/) if you need more help - a trained adviser can give you advice over the phone or by online chat.

If you can’t find any paperwork, try asking your solicitor to check with the previous owner.

If you can’t find the name of the installer or any paperwork, or you can't contact them, you’ll probably have to arrange any repairs or replacements yourself.

### Arranging for repairs or replacements if you own your home

Get a few quotes from different companies to make sure you’re getting the best deal. Always ask if they’ll charge you to come to your home and give you a quote.

[Check how to find an installer for energy efficiency work or repairs on your home](/consumer/energy/energy-supply/save-energy-at-home/find-an-installer-for-energy-efficiency-home-improvements/).

Depending on the company you use, there'll be different payment options available. Check they accept the payment that works best for you - for example, check they accept debit cards if this is how you want to pay.

If you don’t have money to pay for the work now, [check our advice about borrowing money](/debt-and-money/borrowing-money/).

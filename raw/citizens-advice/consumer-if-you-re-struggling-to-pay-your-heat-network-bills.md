#  If you’re struggling to pay your heat network bills

####  If you don’t have a meter

If you think your bill is too high, you should ask your heat network to explain how it’s calculated.

If you can’t reach an agreement, [check how to complain to your heat network supplier](/consumer/energy/energy-supply/problems-with-your-energy-supply/if-theres-a-problem-with-your-heat-network/).

### If your private landlord is overcharging you for heating

Your landlord shouldn't charge you extra for heat if they buy it from the heat network supplier.

If you’re being charged extra, you might be able to take your landlord to court. You’ll need proof - for example, a bill showing the cost of the heating and the extra costs.

For help deciding if you should take your landlord to court, [talk to an adviser](/about-us/contact-us/).

### If you’ve been billed for heat you used a while ago

You might get a bill for heat you’ve used in the past. This is known as ‘back billing’.

If you pay by direct debit, the person or company you pay bills to might increase your payments. They use the extra amount to pay off the amount you owe over a few months.

There might be a limit on how long ago you can be back billed for. This means you might not have to pay for all the heat you’ve used if it was past the limit.

How long the limit is depends on if you pay your heating bills:

  * separately from rent or service charges

  * as part of rent

  * as part of a service charge

####  If you pay your heating bill separately from rent or service charges

Your heat network supplier can usually only send you a bill for heat you used in the last 12 months.

If they send you a bill within this time limit but you don't pay it, they can still charge you for that bill - even after 12 months has passed.

If your supplier doesn’t send you a bill, but later tries to charge you for heat you used more than 12 months ago, you should contact them. Tell them that you’re protected by the back billing rules and should only be charged for heat use within the last 12 months.

If your supplier still tries to charge you the full amount, you should complain. [Check how to complain to your heat network supplier](/consumer/energy/energy-supply/problems-with-your-energy-supply/if-theres-a-problem-with-your-heat-network/).

####  If you pay your heating bill as part of rent

Check your tenancy agreement to see if your heating bill is:

  * the same amount each time

  * a different amount each time, for example a higher amount in winter because your heating is on for longer

##### If you pay the same amount each time

If your landlord tries to increase your rent to cover the higher cost of heating bills, [check how to challenge a rent increase](/housing/paying-rent/challenging-a-rent-increase/).

If you're behind on your rent, you’ll be in ‘arrears’. If your landlord asks you to pay old rent arrears, there’s a time limit on how long they have to get the money back. This is usually 6 years.

You should deal with arrears as fast as you can. You’ll probably be evicted if you don't pay. Even if you can't pay all the money, paying back some money over time might stop you from being evicted.

[Find out how to deal with rent arrears](/housing/paying-rent/dealing-with-rent-arrears-housing/).

##### If you pay a different amount each time

Your landlord can usually only send you a bill for heat you used in the last 18 months.

If they send you a bill within this time limit but you don't pay it, they can still charge you for that bill - even after 18 months has passed.

If your landlord doesn’t send you a bill, but later tries to charge you for heating bills from more than 18 months ago, you should contact them. Tell them that you’re protected by housing law and should only be charged for heat use within the last 18 months.

If they still try to charge you, [talk to an adviser](/about-us/contact-us/).

####  If you pay your heating bill as part of a service charge

Check your contract to see if your heating bill is:

  * the same amount each time

  * a different amount each time, for example a higher amount in winter because your heating is on for longer

##### If you pay the same amount every time

You’ll usually have to pay the bill if your contract says the amount you pay for heating is the same each time.

##### If you pay a different amount each time

Your management company can usually only send you a bill for heat you used in the last 18 months.

If they send you a bill within this time limit but you don't pay it, they can still charge you for that bill - even after 18 months has passed.

If your management company doesn’t send you a bill, but later tries to charge you for a service charge from more than 18 months ago, you should contact them. Tell them that you’re protected by housing law and should only be charged for heat use within the last 18 months.

If they still try to charge you, [talk to an adviser](/about-us/contact-us/).

## Check if you can save money on your energy bills

Your heat network supplier should explain which tariff you’re on, and how it’s calculated. A tariff is a pricing plan for heating. If they haven’t told you about your tariff, contact them and ask.

A tariff includes the rate you’ll pay for the heat you use and a daily fee called a ‘standing charge’. You’ll pay the standing charge no matter how much heat you use.

###  If you pay your energy supplier for your heating

Ask your heat network supplier if they can move you to a cheaper tariff.

Check the details of any new tariff they offer to make sure you'll pay less.

###  If you pay for your heating through your rent or service charge

You probably can’t get a cheaper tariff but it’s worth asking your landlord or property management company.

If you have a meter, you should also check if you can make your home more energy efficient. This means you’ll use less energy and save money on your bills. [Check how to make your home more energy efficient](/consumer/energy/energy-supply/save-energy-at-home/make-sure-your-home-is-energy-efficient/).

You can also [check if you can get grants, benefits or fuel vouchers to help pay your energy bills](/consumer/energy/energy-supply/get-help-paying-your-bills/grants-and-benefits-to-help-you-pay-your-energy-bills/).

## If you can’t afford to top up your prepayment meter

You might be able to get temporary credit if you run out of credit at night, at weekends or on bank holidays. This is called ‘friendly hours credit’ or ‘friendly credit’.

Contact your supplier to check if they can give you friendly hours credit. If they can’t, ask what other help they can give you.

You might also be able to get a fuel voucher. [Check if you can get grants fuel vouchers to help pay your heating bills](/consumer/energy/energy-supply/get-help-paying-your-bills/grants-and-benefits-to-help-you-pay-your-energy-bills/).

### If you run out of temporary credit or can't top up at a top up point

You might be able to get extra temporary credit sometimes called ‘additional support credit’. This credit is a loan from your supplier. You’ll need to agree how to pay it back with your supplier - for example, in monthly payments.

Explain your situation to your supplier. They might give you additional support credit if they consider you as ‘vulnerable’. You might not consider yourself vulnerable, but if you meet the criteria you could get extra help.

You might be classed as vulnerable if you're disabled or have a long-term health condition. This includes hearing, sight and mental health conditions. You might also be classed as vulnerable if you're recovering from an injury.

Your supplier might also class you as vulnerable if you:

  * are over State Pension age - [check your State Pension age on GOV.UK](https://www.gov.uk/state-pension-age)

  * would struggle to answer the door or get help in an emergency

  * are struggling with your living costs - for example, you’re limiting the amount of heating you’re using

  * can’t get to a shop to top up - for example, if you’re ill or injured

  * are pregnant or have children under the age of 5

  * need extra help with communication - for example, if you don’t read or speak English well

If your situation isn't listed, you might still be able to get extra support for other reasons - for example, if you're recently bereaved or you've returned to living independently after being in residential care.

You might still be able to get extra support if your specific situation isn’t listed. Contact your supplier to explain your situation and ask for emergency credit.

### If you keep running out of credit

If it feels like you’re running out of credit too quickly, paying off your heating debt could be the problem. Ask your supplier to let you pay it off in smaller amounts or over more time.

[Contact the Citizens Advice consumer service](/consumer/energy/energy-supply/help/if-you-need-more-help-about-a-consumer-issue-energy/) if you need more help - a trained adviser can give you advice over the phone or online chat.

## Try to agree a plan to pay what you owe

If you think the bill is right but you can't afford to pay it, ask if you can arrange a repayment plan. Your heat network supplier doesn’t have to agree to this, but it’s worth asking.

You can also ask them to freeze any interest and charges as long as you continue to pay the repayment plan.

If you have a prepayment meter, you can pay back some of the money you owe each time you top up your meter.

If you don’t have a prepayment meter, tell your heat network supplier that you want to pay off your debts in ‘instalments’. This means you’ll pay fixed amounts over a set period of time, so you’ll pay what you can afford. Each

payment will include some of what you owe plus an amount for your current use.

Ask your heat network supplier to take into account how much you can afford to pay - give them details about:

  * your income and spending

  * money you owe

  * your personal circumstances

If you’re not sure how much you can afford to pay, you can [use the free budget planner on the MoneyHelper website](https://www.moneyhelper.org.uk/en/everyday-money/budgeting/budget-planner).

### Check if your supplier can install a prepayment meter

Your heat network supplier might want to:

  * install a prepayment meter

  * install a smart meter in prepayment mode

  * remotely switch your smart meter to prepayment mode

They aren’t allowed to do this without your permission. If they do, you should complain.

[Check how to complain to your heat network supplier](/consumer/energy/energy-supply/problems-with-your-energy-supply/if-theres-a-problem-with-your-heat-network/).

If your supplier is trying to move you to prepayment and you don’t think they should, contact them and explain your situation. They should check what support they can offer you. They should also check if you:

  * know how to work a prepayment meter or a smart meter in prepayment mode

  * live far away from a top up point and can’t top up credit remotely

  * can’t get to your meter easily - for example it’s high up, or in a shared cupboard that's locked and you don't have a key

  * need a continuous supply of heat for health reasons

If your supplier still wants to move you to prepayment after you’ve contacted them, you should make a complaint to get them to change their mind.

### If you can’t afford the repayment plan anymore

Speak to your heat network supplier again if your situation has changed and you're struggling to afford the repayments.

You might ask them to:

  * stop contacting you while you work out what to do

  * stop adding interest and charges so the amount you owe doesn't get any bigger

You can also ask your heat network supplier to pause your repayments for a short amount of time. Explain why pausing your repayment will help your situation. For example, tell them if:

  * you’ve lost your job and you’re looking for a new one

  * you’re waiting for a benefit payment

If your heat network supplier agrees to pause your repayments, you should agree how long the pause will be.

If you can't afford the monthly repayments and your supplier has already paused them, ask if they can reduce the amount you repay each month. They should let you spread the repayments over a longer period. Ask them to base the repayment amount on your household income.

## If your heat network supplier says they'll disconnect you

If you miss repayments you agreed to make, your heat network supplier might disconnect your heating and hot water.

Your heat network supplier shouldn’t disconnect you between October and March if it would be unsafe. It’s usually unsafe to disconnect you if:

  * you’re aged 75 or over

  * you live with children aged under 2 years old

  * you need constant supply of heating or hot water for medical reasons

  * you’re pregnant

  * you’re disabled or have a long term health condition

You should contact your heat network to explain your situation. If your situation isn't listed, you should still contact them and explain why it would be unsafe to disconnect you.

If they don’t agree it’s unsafe, you should complain. [Check how to complain to your heat network supplier](/consumer/energy/energy-supply/problems-with-your-energy-supply/if-theres-a-problem-with-your-heat-network/).

## If you’re struggling with living costs

If you’re struggling with money, there are things you can do to save on your regular living costs. [Check what to do if you need help with living costs](/debt-and-money/cost-of-living/get-help-with-the-cost-of-living/).

If you’re finding it hard to pay your bills, you can get help. [Check how to get help with your bills](/debt-and-money/budgeting1/get-help-with-bills/).

If you owe money to more than one company, you can also [get help with debts](/debt-and-money/help-with-debt/dealing-with-your-debts/).

If you're struggling to pay for food, [find out how to get help from a food bank](/debt-and-money/food-bank/using-a-food-bank/).

## If you’re finding things difficult

Your mental health is as important as your physical health. You should talk to your GP if your money problems are affecting your mental health.

You can [find other ways to get help with your mental health on the Mind website](https://www.mind.org.uk/information-support/guides-to-support-and-services/seeking-help-for-a-mental-health-problem/where-to-start/).

### If you need to speak to someone

You can speak to a trained volunteer at organisations like Samaritans or Shout.

Samaritans

Helpline: 116 123 (Monday to Sunday at any time)

Welsh Language Line: 0808 164 0123 (Monday to Sunday 7pm to 11pm)

Calls to Samaritans are free.

You can [find other ways to get in touch with Samaritans on their website](https://www.samaritans.org/how-we-can-help/contact-samaritan/).

#### Shout

You can also text 'SHOUT' to 85258 to start a conversation with a trained Shout 85258 volunteer. Texts are free, anonymous and confidential from anywhere in the UK.

### If you think it's an emergency

If you think your life or someone else’s is at risk, you should call 999 or go to A&E if you can.

You can also [find a list of urgent mental health services on the Mind website](https://www.mind.org.uk/need-urgent-help/finding-out-what-support-is-out-there/).
